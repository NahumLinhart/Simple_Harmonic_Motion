Simple Harmonic Oscillation

A classical harmonic oscillator is a system where when displaced from its equilibrium position, it experiences a force \(F\) that is proportional to its displacement \(x\). A common example is a mass attached to a spring that oscillates about an equilibrium point, shown in figure 6. This relationship is shown in Hooke's law,

\[ F(x) = -k x \]

where \(k\) is the spring constant of the system, a measure of how stiff the spring is. Hooke's law is shown in figure 5, where a red point that corresponds to the movement of the mass in figure 6 travels along the linear curve.

If the system is isolated, that is if \(F\) is the only force acting on the system, then the system is a simple harmonic oscillator and undergoes simple harmonic motion. In simple harmonic motion, the system experiences sinusoidal oscillation with a constant amplitude and period, as seen in figures 1 and 2. The equations used in figures 1 and 2 are in terms of the initial velocity, \(v_0\), which is not the standard way of displaying them. Normally, in simple harmonic oscillation, position as a function of time is written as

\[ x(t) = A\sin(\omega t ) + B\cos(\omega t ) \]

and velocity as a function of time is written as

\[ x'(t) = v(t) = A \omega \cos(\omega t ) - B \omega \sin(\omega t ) \]

where \(A\) and \(B\) are constants that depend on where the block is at \(t = 0\) and

\[ \omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}, \]

the angular frequency. The equations used in this simulation are

\[ x(t) = \frac{v_0}{\omega} \cdot \sin\left(\omega t\right) \]

and

\[ x'(t) = v(t) = v_0 \cdot \cos\left(\omega t\right), \] position and velocity respectively.

These equations are helpful when comparing classical harmonic oscillation to quantum harmonic oscillation, which I hope to showcase in the future. For now, I will not be going over the derivation of the velocity and position equations used, but if you would like to see where they come from check out this video.

Period and Frequency in Simple Harmonic Motion

The period (\(T\)) is the time in seconds it takes for an oscillitory system to complete one oscillation. In a mass-spring system the period is expressed as

\[ T = 2\pi\sqrt\frac{m}{k} \]

and is held constant in simple harmonic motion.

The frequency (\(f\)) is the amount of oscillations the system completes per unit time. The standard unit is hertz (Hz), the amount oscillations completed per second. Frequency is expressed as

\[ f = \frac{1}{T} = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt\frac{k}{m} \]

and is also held constant in simple harmonic motion.

Elastic Potential Energy in Simple Harmonic Motion

Elastic potential energy in a simple harmonic oscillator is modeled by the equation

\[ U(x) = \frac{1}{2} k x^2. \]

This relationship is shown in figure 3, where a red ball oscillates about \(x = 0\), the equilibrium point of the system. The total energy of the system is

\[ E_{total} = \frac{1}{2} m v_0 ^2. \]

To derive the total energy, we sum the potential energy and the kinetic energy. We know that kinetic energy is

\[ K = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 = \frac{1}{2} m \cdot (v_0 \cdot \cos\left(\omega t\right))^2 \]

and that the potential energy is

\[ U = \frac{1}{2} k x^2 = \frac{1}{2} k \cdot (\frac{v_0}{\omega} \cdot \sin\left(\omega t\right))^2. \]

Summing these, we get

\[ E_{total} = \frac{1}{2} m v_0^2 \cdot \cos^2\left(\omega t\right) + \frac{1}{2} m v_0^2 \cdot \sin^2\left(\omega t\right). \]

We can factor out the \(\frac{1}{2} m v_0^2\), getting

\[ E_{total} = \frac{1}{2} m v_0^2 \cdot (\cos^2\left(\omega t\right) + \sin^2\left(\omega t\right)). \]

Recall that

\[ \sin^2(\theta) + \cos^2(\theta) = 1 \]

so we can simplify our equation to

\[ E_{total} = \frac{1}{2} m v_0 ^2. \]

The total energy is represented as a horizontal line that intersects the parabola at \[ x = \pm \sqrt{\frac{m v_0^2}{k}} .\]

The relationship between the kinetic energy and potential energy of a system in simple harmonic motion is shown in figures 3 and 4. Like the red point on figure 5, the red points on figures 3 and 4 correspond to the movement of the mass in figure 6. When the red point is at \(x=0\), the potential energy of the system is zero. At the same time, the velocity of the system is at its greatest magnitude. Conversely, when the system is at its maximum displacement, the potential energy equals the total energy while the velocity equals zero. Since velocity is proportional to kinetic energy, we notice that as the potential energy decreases, the kinetic energy increases, and vice versa. Figures 3 and 4 showcase conservation of energy: the system oscillates from the total energy being equal to kinetic energy to the total energy being potential energy.

Applications of Simple Harmonic Motion

When a system has a complex potential curve \(U(x)\) with multiple stable equilibrium points, as found in most physical systems, the potential in simple harmonic motion can become quite useful. Any potential curve can be expanded in powers of x around a stable equilibrium point using a Taylor series,

\[ U(x-x_0) = U(x_0) + \frac{1}{2!} \cdot U''(x_0)(x-x_0)^2 + \frac{1}{3!} \cdot U'''(x_0)(x-x_0)^3... \]

where \(x_0\) is an equilibrium point. \(U'(x_0) = 0,\) so we can ignore that term.

Now, if we take \(x_0\) to be \(0\), we get

\[ U(x) = U(0) + \frac{1}{2!} \cdot U''(0)(x)^2 ... \]

As you can see, the first three terms are just a parabola, like elastic potential in simple harmonic motion. Since \(x_0\) is a minimum, \(U'\) goes from negative to positive about \(x_0\), meaning \(U'' > 0\). This means that \(U''(0)\) is a positive constant, which we can treat as the spring constant of a system in simple harmonic motion. Through using a Taylor series, we've shown that any potential, no matter how complicated, will reduce to a simple harmonic oscillator in the vicinity of a stable equilibrium point.
